### Overview

The Minnesota Sex Trafficking Prevention Training is a critical initiative designed to help hotels, motels, and short-term rentals in the state recognize and respond to incidents of sex trafficking. This legislation requires all owners, managers, and on-site employees of these establishments to complete the training within specified timeframes.

#### Key Points:

1. **Required Participants:**
   - All hotel, motel, and short-term rental owners, managers, and on-site employees must complete the training.
   - Exemptions include employees under 16 years old unless they work in housekeeping, those working exclusively in restaurants or food service, and those with no direct contact with guests.

2. **Timing:**
   - New employees must take the training within 90 days of hire.
   - All required staff must retake the training annually.

3. **Training Content:**
   - The training covers identifying signs of sex trafficking and what to do if such incidents are suspected or reported.
   - It includes a virtual presentation, a training companion guide, and a manager/owner toolkit.

4. **Implementation:**
   - Licensees (hotel owners) are responsible for ensuring compliance with the law, including printing required posters and conducting ongoing awareness campaigns.
   - The Minnesota Department of Health (MDH) provides free training materials but does not cover costs associated with compliance, such as printing posters.

5. **Language Availability:**
   - Training materials will be available in English, Spanish, Hmong, and Somali starting late fall 2023/early 2024.
   - If additional languages are needed, the MDH Safe Harbor Program can assist by providing materials or directing individuals to contact health.safeharbor@state.mn.us.

6. **Monitoring and Reporting:**
   - Licensees must maintain a roster of employees who have completed the training.
   - There is no requirement to send this roster to MDH for verification; compliance can be demonstrated through records kept on-site.

### Compliance Steps

1. **Access Training Materials:**
   - Managers and owners need to access the training package via the MDH Learning Center, completing a pre-training survey before gaining access.
   - Employees can access the training in their preferred language (English, Spanish, Hmong, or Somali).

2. **Completion of Training:**
   - The training is expected to take approximately 45 to 50 minutes.
   - Access materials and resources provided by MDH, including a virtual presentation with guided exercises.

3. **Display the Required Poster:**
   - Posters must be displayed in key locations such as back-of-house, housekeeping, restaurant/bar, front desk/lobby, and security areas.

4. **Conduct Awareness Campaigns:**
   - Regular awareness campaigns should be conducted for all employees to reinforce training objectives.

5. **Maintain Records:**
   - Maintain a roster of completed trainings with dates for each employee.
   - Store this information on-site for inspection purposes without sending it to MDH.

### FAQs

1. **Do I still need to take the training if I've taken a previous sex trafficking course?**
   - Yes, you must complete the Minnesota-specific training package to comply with the law.

2. **Are resorts, bed and breakfasts, vacation shares, and short-term rentals required to provide the training?**
   - Only those holding hotel licenses are required to provide the training.

3. **Does the law apply to tribal nations?**
   - No, but MDH encourages tribes to provide the training.

4. **Are there any employees exempt from the training?**
   - Exemptions include employees under 16 unless they work in housekeeping, those working exclusively in restaurants or food service, and those with no direct contact with guests.

5. **What if I'm unsure about my license status?**
   - Contact health.foodlodging@state.mn.us or 651-201-4500 for clarification.

### Conclusion

The Minnesota Sex Trafficking Prevention Training is a vital step in combating sex trafficking within the hospitality industry. By adhering to the training requirements and maintaining compliance, establishments can help protect their communities and contribute to national efforts against this heinous crime.
