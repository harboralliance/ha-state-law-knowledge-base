**SB1422 - Human Trafficking Prevention and Awareness Act**

This legislation, **Senate Bill 1422**, was introduced in the Illinois General Assembly on June 1, 2020. The bill aims to enhance public awareness about human trafficking and prevent it within lodging establishments, restaurants, and truck stops by requiring mandatory training for employees.

### Key Sections of SB1422

#### **Section 5: Definitions**

- **Department:** Refers to the Department of Human Services.
- **Employee:** Includes persons employed in lodging establishments, restaurants, or truck stops who interact with the public regularly.
- **Employer:** A person or entity operating a lodging establishment, restaurant, or truck stop.
- **Human Trafficking:** Defined as depriving or violating personal liberty for forced labor, commercial sex, or other exploitation.
- **Lodging Establishment:** Includes hotels and motels classified under the North American Industry Classification System (NAICS) codes 721110 and 721120, as well as casino hotels.
- **Restaurant:** A business primarily engaged in selling ready-to-eat food for immediate consumption comprising at least 51% of total sales.
- **Truck Stop:** An establishment providing services to the trucking industry.

#### **Section 10: Human Trafficking Recognition Training**

- Effective June 1, 2020, employers must provide their employees with training on human trafficking recognition and reporting protocols within six months of beginning employment in a relevant role.
- Employees must complete this training every two years if they continue to work in the same role.

#### **Section 15: Human Trafficking Recognition Training Curriculum**

- Employers can use either their own program or one developed by a third party, as long as it meets minimum requirements including definitions of human trafficking, guidance on identifying high-risk individuals, differences between labor and sex trafficking, and roles in reporting and responding to trafficking incidents.
- The Department of Human Services must develop a comprehensive curriculum for approved programs if an employer chooses not to use their own program. This curriculum must cover the same topics as required for third-party programs.

#### **Section 20: Penalties**

- Local government units or law enforcement agencies can monitor and enforce compliance with this Act.
- Upon discovering a violation, these entities must provide a reasonable notice of noncompliance.
- If a violation is not corrected within 30 days, the Attorney General or State's Attorney may file a civil action against the employer.
- Employers found guilty of violating the Act are subject to a fine of up to $1,500 per offense.

### Effective Date

The bill takes effect on January 1, 2026. This timeline allows ample time for employers to implement training programs and ensure compliance before the mandatory provisions kick in.

### Impact and Purpose

The primary goal of SB1422 is to protect vulnerable individuals from exploitation and trafficking within the hospitality industry. By requiring regular human trafficking training for employees, the bill aims to enhance their ability to identify potential victims and take appropriate actions to prevent trafficking incidents. This legislation aligns with national efforts to combat human trafficking and support survivors while also safeguarding businesses through responsible labor practices.

### Conclusion

SB1422 is a significant step towards preventing human trafficking within the hospitality sector. It establishes mandatory training requirements, develops comprehensive curricula, and imposes penalties for non-compliance. The bill's long-term effectiveness will depend on how well it is implemented and enforced, ensuring that both employers and employees are fully prepared to address the complex issue of human trafficking.
