## Summary
Connecticut has enacted several laws to address human trafficking, including sex and labor. The state requires annual training for hotels, motels, and lodging businesses regarding human trafficking. Additionally, the law mandates mandatory data reporting and a statewide oversight and monitoring body.

## Extracted Requirements
- Applicable industry: Hotels, motels, inns, similar lodging
- Training required: Annual human trafficking training
- Signage required: None explicitly mentioned in the legislation
- Reporting required: Mandatory data reporting
- Enforcement agency: Department of Public Health (DPH), law enforcement
- Penalties: Class C felony for commercial sexual exploitation of a minor
- Effective date: Various laws have different effective dates, ranging from 2010 to 2025

## Harbor Alliance Impact
- Course impact: Enhances knowledge and awareness among service providers, foster parents, schools, law enforcement, hospital and medical staff, hospitality industry professionals, and the community.
- Signage impact: Not explicitly mentioned in the legislation, but could be a potential area for awareness campaigns.

## Legal Framework
- 2017 Public Act 17-32 (PDF): Defines human trafficking as a crime with severe penalties.
- 2016 Public Act 16-71 (PDF): Expands the definition of human trafficking and requires state attorneys and local law enforcement to document relevant training and investigations.
- 2015 Public Act 15-195 (PDF): Expands protections for victims of human trafficking, including erasure of juvenile records and wiretapping authorization.
- 2014 Public Act 14-186 (PDF): Provides services to human trafficking victims classified as "uncared for" and requires DCF training on human trafficking.
- 2013 Public Act 13-166 (PDF): Aligns with the federal Trafficking Victims Protection Act (TVPA), making commercial sexual exploitation of a minor a class C felony.
- 2012 Public Act 12-141 (PDF): Defines commercial sexual exploitation of a minor as a crime and imposes significant penalties.
- 2011 Public Act 11-180 (PDF): Requires DCF to report suspected abuse or neglect when youth are arrested for prostitution.
- 2010 Public Act 10-115 (PDF): Increases the minimum age for prostitution from 16 to 18 and provides presumptions in prosecutions involving minors.

## Conclusion
Connecticut has a comprehensive legislative framework aimed at preventing and combating human trafficking. Annual training requirements for businesses, mandatory data reporting, and a statewide oversight body are key components of these efforts. However, additional signage campaigns and prevention-focused initiatives could further enhance awareness and deterrence.
