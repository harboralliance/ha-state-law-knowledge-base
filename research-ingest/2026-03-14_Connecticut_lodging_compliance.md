## Summary
Connecticut has implemented various measures to combat human trafficking, including providing training for service providers and establishing legislation aimed at enhancing protections for victims. The state offers specific trainings for foster parents, law enforcement, healthcare providers, and the hospitality industry.

## Extracted Requirements
- **Applicable industry:** Foster care services, law enforcement, healthcare providers, hotel and motel industry.
- **Training required:** Introduction to Child Trafficking in Connecticut, Introduction to Human Trafficking in Connecticut for Law Enforcement (POST Certified), Child Trafficking Training for Medical Providers/Emergency Medicine, Introduction to Labor Trafficking in Connecticut.
- **Signage required:** No specific signage requirements mentioned.
- **Reporting required:** Yes, mandatory data reporting is required as part of human trafficking legislation.
- **Enforcement agency:** Department of Children and Families (DCF), law enforcement agencies.
- **Penalties:** Violations are subject to fines and potential criminal penalties, including class C felonies for commercial sexual exploitation of a minor and other forms of human trafficking.
- **Effective date:** Legislation passed from 2010 onwards with the most recent updates in 2017.

## Effective Date
Connecticut's human trafficking legislation became effective starting from 2010 with subsequent amendments up to 2017.

### Detailed Analysis

#### Trainings for Providers
- **Foster Care:** Foster parents are required to receive specific training on identifying and addressing child trafficking.
- **Law Enforcement:** Law enforcement agencies must undergo specialized training in human trafficking, including handling missing children cases and referrals for prosecution.
- **Healthcare Providers:** Healthcare providers need to be trained to recognize signs of human trafficking and provide appropriate care to victims.
- **Hotel and Motel Industry:** All employees in hotels and motels are required to receive annual trafficking training and awareness campaigns.

#### Legislation
- **Public Act 17-32 (2017):** Expands the conditions under which human trafficking is committed, particularly for minors, and requires DPH to provide services similar to sexual assault victims.
- **Public Act 16-71 (2016):** Aids in documenting state attorneys' and law enforcement's involvement in trafficking cases.
- **Public Act 15-195 (2015):** Expands protections for human trafficking victims by waiving crime victim compensation application limits for minors.
- **Public Act 14-186 (2014):** Provides DCF with services for unattended human trafficking victims and establishes multidisciplinary teams for handling cases.
- **Public Act 13-166 (2013):** Aligns with the federal Trafficking Victims Protection Act, making commercial sexual exploitation of a minor a class C felony.
- **Public Act 12-141 (2012):** Defines commercial sexual exploitation of a minor and makes it a class C felony.
- **Public Act 11-180 (2011):** Requires reporting suspected abuse or neglect to DCF when arrested for prostitution.
- **Public Act 10-115 (2010):** Sets the minimum age for prostitution at sixteen and provides presumptions of coercion.

#### Reporting
- **Mandatory Data Reporting:** Human trafficking cases must be reported to state authorities, ensuring ongoing monitoring and combating efforts.

### Conclusion
Connecticut's approach to combating human trafficking is multifaceted, focusing on training service providers, implementing stringent legislation, and ensuring mandatory reporting. These measures are designed to protect victims and enhance the response from various stakeholders within the state.
