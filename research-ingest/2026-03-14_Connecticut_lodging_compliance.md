## Summary
Connecticut has comprehensive legislation and training programs in place to address human trafficking, including sex and labor trafficking. The state requires various groups to receive training and mandates certain practices to combat the issue.

## Extracted Requirements
- **Applicable industry:** Hotels, motels, inns, and similar lodging; law enforcement; foster care services; DCF staff; schools.
- **Training required:**
  - Introduction to Child Trafficking in Connecticut (2.5 hours)
  - Introduction to Human Trafficking in Connecticut for School Settings (2 hours)
  - Introduction to Human Trafficking in Connecticut for Law Enforcement - Police Officer's Standards and Training (POST) Certified (2.5 hours)
  - Introduction to Human Trafficking in Connecticut for Emergency Medical Services (EMS) (2 hours)
  - Child Trafficking Training for Medical Providers/Emergency Medicine (1-2 hours)
  - Introduction to Human Trafficking in Connecticut for Hotels, Motels, and Lodging (1.5 hours)
  - Mylifemychoce.org’s My Life My Choice Curriculum (10 sessions of 75 minutes each)
- **Significant legislation:**
  - Public Act 17-32 (2017): An Act Concerning Human Trafficking
  - Public Act 16-71 (2016): An Act Concerning Human Trafficking
  - Public Act 15-195 (2015): An Act Strengthening Protections for Victims of Human Trafficking
  - Public Act 14-186 (2014): An Act Concerning the Department of Children and Families and the Protection of Children
  - Public Act 13-166 (2013): An Act Concerning Sexual Exploitation and Trafficking in Persons
  - Public Act 12-141 (2012): An Act Concerning Commercial Sexual Exploitation of a Minor
  - Public Act 11-180 (2011): An Act Concerning Notification by the Department of Children and Families When a Youth is Arrested for Prostitution and Out-of-State Placements of Children and Youth.
  - Public Act 10-115 (2010): An Act Providing a Safe Harbor for Exploited Children

## Enforcement Measures
- **Training requirements:** Hotels, motels, inns, and similar lodging must provide annual trafficking training to all employees.
- **Guest records:** Guest transactions and receipts must be kept for at least 6 months.
- **Data reporting:** Ongoing monitoring of efforts to combat trafficking and mandatory data reporting.

## Legal Penalties
- A person must be eighteen years of age or older to be guilty of prostitution.
- Commercial sexual exploitation of a minor is a class C felony.
- Expanding protections for victims of human trafficking, including erasure of police and court records for minors, and wiretapping authorizations.

## Conclusion
Connecticut's efforts in combating human trafficking are robust, encompassing comprehensive legislation and mandatory training across multiple sectors. The state's proactive measures aim to protect vulnerable populations and hold perpetrators accountable through stringent legal penalties.
