Connecticut

## Summary
Connecticut has comprehensive laws and training programs in place to address human trafficking, particularly sex trafficking. The state requires annual basic training for all Child Protective Services staff on human trafficking. Advanced training can be taken instead. Incoming staff must be trained within six months of hire. Various industry groups are also required to receive specific trainings tailored to their roles.

## Extracted Requirements
- Applicable industry: DCF staff, foster parents, service providers, schools, law enforcement, hospital and medical staff, hospitality industry, other professionals, youth, and the community.
- Training required: Annual basic training for Child Protective Services staff; advanced training available. Specific trainings for various industry groups (e.g., hotels, motels, emergency medical services).
- Signage required: Not explicitly mentioned in the provided sources.
- Reporting required: State attorneys and local law enforcement must document training, investigations of missing children, trafficking referrals, trafficking cases referred for prosecution, etc.
- Enforcement agency: Department of Children and Families (DCF), state attorneys, local law enforcement.
- Penalties: Commercial sexual exploitation of a minor is a class C felony; human trafficking is subject to specific provisions in the 2016 Public Act 16-71.

## Effective Measures
Connecticut has enacted multiple public acts that strengthen protections for victims and expand the crime of human trafficking. These include expanding the conditions under which human trafficking can be committed, providing services to human trafficking victims classified as “uncared for,” creating multidisciplinary teams to work with cases, and establishing a statewide oversight and monitoring body.

## Potential Improvements
While Connecticut has robust legislation in place, there is room for improvement in ensuring consistent enforcement and public awareness. Enhanced data reporting mechanisms and increased funding for victim services could further strengthen the state's response to human trafficking.
