## Summary
Connecticut has enacted comprehensive legislation to address human trafficking, including both sex and labor trafficking. The state requires training for various groups such as DCF staff, foster parents, service providers, schools, law enforcement, hospitals, hospitality industry professionals, and other professionals. Training is mandatory for all Child Protective Services staff annually and for incoming staff within six months of hire. Additionally, the state has developed specific training programs for school settings, law enforcement, emergency medical services (EMS), hotels, motels, and lodging businesses.

## Extracted Requirements
- **Applicable industry:** DCF staff, foster parents, service providers, schools, law enforcement, hospitals, hospitality industry professionals, other professionals, youth, and the community.
- **Training required:** Annual basic training on human trafficking for all Child Protective Services staff. Advanced training is available instead of the basic one.
- **Signage required:** No specific signage requirements mentioned in the provided sources.
- **Reporting required:** State attorneys and local law enforcement must document training, investigations of missing children, trafficking referrals, trafficking cases referred for prosecution, etc.
- **Enforcement measures:** Hotels, motels, inns, and similar lodging must provide trafficking training and awareness campaigns to all employees annually. They must also keep guest transactions and receipts for at least 6 months.
- **Victim services:** DCF provides services to human trafficking victims classified as “uncared for.” The Department of Public Health (DPH) is required to provide the same services it must provide certain sexual assault victims under existing law.

## Enforcement and Monitoring
Connecticut requires mandatory data reporting, including information about human trafficking cases. There is a statewide oversight and monitoring body that helps combat trafficking efforts continuously. Additionally, specific laws allow for affirmative defense for minors in trafficking cases and enable the Office of Victim Services to waive the two-year limitation on crime victim compensation applications for minors who are victims of human trafficking.

## Legislation
Connecticut has enacted several public acts addressing human trafficking, including:
- **2017 Public Act 17-32**: An Act Concerning Human Trafficking
- **2016 Public Act 16-71**: An Act Concerning Human Trafficking
- **2015 Public Act 15-195**: An Act Strengthening Protections for Victims of Human Trafficking
- **2014 Public Act 14-186**: An Act Concerning the Department of Children and Families and the Protection of Children
- **2013 Public Act 13-166**: An Act Concerning Sexual Exploitation and Trafficking in Persons
- **2012 Public Act 12-141**: An Act Concerning Commercial Sexual Exploitation of a Minor
- **2011 Public Act 11-180**: An Act Concerning Notification by the Deparment of Children and Families When a Youth is Arrested for Prostitution and Out-of-State Placements of Children and Youth.
- **2010 Public Act 10-115**: An Act Providing a Safe Harbor for Exploited Children

These laws ensure that human trafficking victims receive necessary services, and the state maintains comprehensive oversight to combat this issue effectively.

## Conclusion
Connecticut's approach to combating human trafficking is multifaceted, involving legislative efforts, mandatory training programs, and data reporting mechanisms. The state's commitment to providing comprehensive support and monitoring ensures that individuals affected by human trafficking are adequately assisted while law enforcement agencies are equipped with the necessary tools to address these crimes effectively.
