# State Research Capture

## State
Connecticut

## Summary
Connecticut has a robust framework for addressing human trafficking, including training programs for various professionals and legislation that expands protections for victims and strengthens penalties. The state requires annual basic training for all Child Protective Services staff and specific training for foster parents, schools, law enforcement, emergency medical services, hotels, motels, and lodging businesses.

## Extracted Requirements
- Applicable industry: All service providers, including DCF staff, foster parents, schools, law enforcement, hospital and medical staff, hospitality industry professionals, youth, and the community.
- Training required: Basic training for all Child Protective Services staff annually; specific trainings for foster parents, schools, law enforcement (POST Certified), emergency medical services, hotels, motels, and lodging businesses.
- Signage required: Not explicitly mentioned in the sources.
- Reporting required: State attorneys and local law enforcement must document training, investigations of missing children, trafficking referrals, trafficking cases referred for prosecution, etc. Hotels, motels, inns, and similar lodging must keep guest transactions and receipts for at least 6 months.
- Enforcement: Ongoing monitoring of efforts to combat trafficking and mandatory data reporting.

## Enforcement
Connecticut's human trafficking legislation includes provisions for mandatory data reporting and ongoing monitoring by a statewide oversight and monitoring body. The state also allows the Office of Victim Services (OVS) to waive the two-year limitation on crime victim compensation applications for minors who are victims of human trafficking.

## Conclusion
Connecticut takes a comprehensive approach to human trafficking, providing essential training to various stakeholders and implementing robust legislation to protect victims and strengthen penalties for perpetrators.
