The provided text is a legislation document for the State of Illinois, specifically aimed at preventing human trafficking in lodging establishments, restaurants, and truck stops. Here's a breakdown of its key points:

1. **Definitions**: The Act defines various terms including "Department" (Department of Human Services), "Employee" (person employed by lodging, restaurant, or truck stop with recurring public interactions), "Employer" (person or entity operating these establishments), "Human trafficking," and specific classifications for "Lodging establishment," "Restaurant," and "Truck stop."

2. **Training Requirement**: Effective June 1, 2020, employers in lodging, restaurants, and truck stops must provide their employees with training on human trafficking recognition and reporting protocols. Employees are required to complete this training within six months of starting the job and every two years thereafter.

3. **Training Curriculum**: Employers can use either their own human trafficking training program or a third-party program approved by the Department. The curriculum must cover at least four key areas: 
   - Definition of human trafficking and commercial exploitation of children.
   - Identification of individuals most at risk for human trafficking.
   - Differences in human trafficking related to lodging, restaurants, and truck stops (for labor vs. sex).
   - Roles of employees in reporting and responding to human trafficking.

4. **Department's Curriculum**: If an employer does not use a third-party approved program, the Department must develop and publish a curriculum by July 1, 2020. The Department’s curriculum should include all the required training topics. Additionally, they may consult with the U.S. Department of Justice for assistance.

5. **Penalties**: A unit of local government or law enforcement agency can monitor compliance with this act and issue warnings if a violation is discovered. If the violation isn't corrected within 30 days, the Attorney General or State's Attorney can bring a civil action against the employer. The maximum fine for each offense is $1,500.

6. **Effective Date**: The Act takes effect on January 1, 2026.

This legislation aims to increase awareness and response capabilities within the hospitality sector to help combat human trafficking effectively.
