**Title: Enhanced Human Trafficking Prevention in Lodging Establishments, Restaurants, and Truck Stops - Act 2026**

---

### **Section I: Definitions**
- **Department:** The Department of Human Services.
- **Employee:** A person employed by a lodging establishment, restaurant, or truck stop who has recurring interactions with the public.
- **Employer:** A person or entity that operates a lodging establishment, restaurant, or truck stop.
- **Human Trafficking:** The deprivation or violation of another's personal liberty with the intent to obtain forced labor or services, procure or sell individuals for commercial sex, or exploit individuals in obscene matter.
- **Lodging Establishment:** An establishment classified as a hotel or motel under the 2017 North American Industry Classification System.
- **Restaurant:** A business primarily engaged in selling ready-to-eat food for immediate consumption comprising at least 51% of total sales.
- **Truck Stop:** An establishment intended to provide services to the trucking industry.

---

### **Section II: Human Trafficking Recognition Training**
- **Effective Date:** June 1, 2020
- **Requirement:** Employers must provide their employees with training in human trafficking recognition and protocols for reporting observed instances. Employees must complete this training within 6 months of starting employment in a relevant role and every 2 years thereafter.
- **Duration:** Training shall be at least 20 minutes long.

---

### **Section III: Human Trafficking Recognition Training Curriculum**
- **Customizable Programs:** Employers may use their own human trafficking training program or that of a third party, as long as it includes:
  1. A definition of human trafficking and commercial exploitation of children.
  2. Guidance on identifying individuals at risk for human trafficking.
  3. The distinction between human trafficking for labor versus sex purposes relevant to lodging establishments, restaurants, and truck stops.
  4. Guidance on employees' roles in reporting and responding to instances of human trafficking.
- **Departmental Curriculum:** The Department shall develop a model curriculum for approved human trafficking recognition programs which must be used by entities not administering their own program. This includes all the requirements mentioned above. The Department may consult with the U.S. Department of Justice on this development.
- **Publication Deadline:** The department shall publish this training program no later than July 1, 2020.

---

### **Section IV: Penalties**
- **Local Government or Law Enforcement Monitoring:** A unit of local government regulating an employer or a law enforcement agency with jurisdiction over the employer may monitor and enforce compliance with this Act.
- **Notice of Noncompliance:** Upon discovery of a violation, the relevant body must provide reasonable notice to the employer informing them that non-compliance will lead to a civil penalty if not corrected within 30 days.
- **Civil Action by Attorney General/State’s Attorney:** If the violation is not corrected within this period, the Attorney General or State’s Attorney may bring a civil action against the employer. An employer found in violation of this Act is guilty of a business offense and faces fines up to $1,500 per offense.

---

### **Section V: Effective Date**
- This Act takes effect on January 1, 2026.

---

**Summary:** The Act aims to strengthen measures against human trafficking by mandating annual human trafficking recognition training for employees in lodging establishments, restaurants, and truck stops. Employers must provide this training to all relevant staff within six months of their employment and every two years thereafter. Non-compliance could result in fines up to $1,500 per violation. The Department is tasked with developing a model curriculum which will be made available for use by entities not administering their own programs.
